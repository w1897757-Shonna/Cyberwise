Key message: Scam letters are designed to pressure you into sending money, calling expensive numbers, or sharing personal details.

What you learned

  • Scammers may send fake prize or postal lottery letters saying you have won money.
  • They may ask for an admin fee, payment, or premium-rate phone call to claim the prize.
  • Some letters pretend to be bills, solicitor letters, psychic offers, or inheritance claims.
  • These scams often look official to make them seem real.
  • You should never send money or personal details in response to suspicious mail.
Example scam scenario

You receive a letter saying you have won a large prize in an international postal lottery. The letter looks official and includes stamps, reference numbers, and a congratulatory message. But before you can claim the money, it asks you to send a small processing fee or call a premium-rate number. This is a scam. If you did not enter the lottery, you did not win it.

How to protect yourself
  • Throw away suspicious letters and do not reply.
  • Never send money to claim a prize or inheritance.
  • Do not call premium-rate numbers, especially those starting with 09.
  • Check organisations using official websites or trusted phone numbers.
  • Ask someone you trust if you are unsure.
  • Report scam mail to Royal Mail or Citizens Advice.
